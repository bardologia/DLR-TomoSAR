\section{Full derivation of the patch-size law}
\label{app:patch_law}

This appendix derives the context ceiling stated in Section~\ref{sec:method_patch}, develops the sample-budget account that was registered against it, and reports the sweep that adjudicates between the two. Throughout, $w$ is the side of the boxcar covariance window used by the labelling pipeline ($w = 20$\,px in production), $N$ the number of passes in the labelling stack ($N = 29$), $n \le N$ the number of passes in the network input, $W$ the input patch side, and $\tau = (\tau_a, \tau_r)$ a pixel offset in azimuth and range.

\subsection{Assumptions}
\label{app:patch_assumptions}

\begin{itemize}
  \item[\textbf{A1}] \emph{Speckle decorrelation at the resolution-cell scale.} Sample vectors at distinct pixels are statistically independent. Fully developed speckle satisfies this; organised long-range structure weakens it (Appendix~\ref{app:patch_limits}).
  \item[\textbf{A2}] \emph{Homogeneity at the window scale.} The true covariance $\mathbf{R}(\cdot)$ is approximately constant over any $w \times w$ window. This is not an additional hypothesis: the labelling pipeline itself relies on it, since the boxcar average of Eq.~(\ref{eq:label_cov}) estimates $\mathbf{R}(x)$ consistently only where $\mathbf{R}$ is locally constant, and $w$ is chosen as the homogeneity scale of the scene.
  \item[\textbf{A3}] \emph{Equal per-pass quality.} All passes carry comparable SNR and coherence (one campaign, one sensor).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{The label correlation law}
\label{app:patch_correlation}

The label at pixel $x$ is $\ell(x) = \phi\bigl(\hat{\mathbf{R}}(x)\bigr)$, where $\phi$ denotes the deterministic spectral scan and mixture fit applied to the windowed sample covariance of Eq.~(\ref{eq:label_cov}). Three consequences follow.

\textbf{Support.} $\ell(x)$ is a function of the samples in $B(x)$ alone; nothing outside the window enters the label.

\textbf{Exact independence beyond $w$.} If $|\tau_a| \ge w$ or $|\tau_r| \ge w$, then $B(x) \cap B(x+\tau) = \emptyset$, and under A1 the labels $\ell(x)$ and $\ell(x+\tau)$ are functions of disjoint independent sample sets --- independent, not merely uncorrelated. Two disjoint boxcar averages of independent speckle share nothing.

\textbf{Triangular correlation within $w$.} The windows share
\begin{equation}
  \bigl|B(x) \cap B(x+\tau)\bigr| \;=\; (w - |\tau_a|)_+\,(w - |\tau_r|)_+
  \label{eq:shared_count}
\end{equation}
samples. Under A1--A2 the covariance between corresponding entries of $\hat{\mathbf{R}}(x)$ and $\hat{\mathbf{R}}(x+\tau)$ is proportional to this count, so the entry-wise correlation is the separable triangle
\begin{equation}
  \rho(\tau) \;=\; \Bigl(1 - \tfrac{|\tau_a|}{w}\Bigr)_{\!+}\Bigl(1 - \tfrac{|\tau_r|}{w}\Bigr)_{\!+},
  \label{eq:triangle}
\end{equation}
the self-convolution of the boxcar, and the labels inherit it to first order in the linearisation of $\phi$ (delta method). The exact zero of the previous paragraph does not depend on this linearisation.

\subsection{The ceiling}
\label{app:patch_ceiling}

Held-out risk separates the two things a predictor could chase. Writing $\ell(x) = \phi(\mathbf{R}(x)) + \varepsilon(x)$, with $\varepsilon$ the estimation noise of the finite $w^2$-look budget, the test loss of a predictor $f$ decomposes as $\E \norm{f - \ell}^2 = \E \norm{f - \phi(\mathbf{R})}^2 + \E \norm{\varepsilon}^2$: the reducible part is the distance to the underlying statistic, not to the label realisation. A larger patch can lower it only by supplying additional looks that are informative about $\mathbf{R}(x)$, and the label construction itself certifies which looks those are. Pixels within $w$ of $x$ are anchored to the label through shared samples: their windows overlap $B(x)$, the corresponding covariance estimates are correlated (Eq.~\ref{eq:triangle}), and --- because overlapping homogeneity windows must agree on their intersection (A2) --- their remaining looks sample the same $\mathbf{R}(x)$, averaging away $\varepsilon$. Pixels farther than $w$ are, under A1, exactly independent of every sample that formed $\ell(x)$; any contribution from them rests on extrapolating homogeneity across windows that share no data --- a scene prior the label construction neither uses nor certifies.

The certified context is therefore the square of side $2w$ centred on $x$, and a patch of side $W$ captures
\begin{equation}
  L(W) \;=\; \min(W, 2w)^2
  \label{eq:ceiling}
\end{equation}
of it: the pooled look count grows quadratically until $W = 2w$ and is exactly flat beyond. The law follows --- held-out accuracy improves with patch size up to $W = 2w$ and saturates, independently of $n$, since Eq.~(\ref{eq:ceiling}) contains no property of the input stack.

Two neighbouring readings bracket this one and make the experiment sharp. If the network needed only to \emph{reproduce the label realisation}, the patch would need to contain no more than the label's own inputs, and accuracy would saturate at $W = w$. If homogeneity could instead be \emph{chained} across successively overlapping windows, information would accrue without bound and no saturation would appear. The three readings predict knees at $w$, at $2w$, and nowhere; a sweep distinguishes them.

\subsection{The registered alternative --- a sample-budget floor}
\label{app:patch_floor}

A competing account of the optimum was registered alongside the ceiling before the sweep; we develop it in full because rejecting it is part of the evidence for the law.

The labels are manufactured from $N$ passes; the network input contains $n < N$. One independent speckle look occupies $o_a \times o_r$ pixels, where $o_a = \rho_a/\delta_a$ and $o_r = \rho_r/\delta_r$ are the azimuth and range oversampling factors (resolution over pixel spacing). The labelling covariance therefore pools $L = w^2/(o_a o_r)$ looks over $N$ passes --- in production $L \approx 121 \approx 4.2\,N$, comfortably above the $L \ge 2N$ Reed--Mallett--Brennan soundness bound. If estimation information scales as looks $\times$ passes, an $n$-pass input must repay the label's budget with area, $L' = L\,N/n$ looks, which converts back to pixel area as
\begin{equation}
  A_{\mathrm{ctx}} \;=\; L'\,o_a o_r \;=\; \frac{N}{n}\,w^2
  \quad\Longrightarrow\quad
  W^{*} \;=\; w\sqrt{N/n},
  \label{eq:floor}
\end{equation}
with the oversampling factors cancelling exactly: the floor depends only on the window and the pass ratio.

Pass count is, moreover, a coarse currency, and the floor sharpens under a Fisher-information account. Elevation enters pass $i$ only through the phase $e^{\mathrm{j} k_z^{(i)} z}$, so estimating $z$ is single-tone frequency estimation with the vertical wavenumbers $k_z^{(i)}$ as sample positions; with the common complex amplitude a nuisance parameter, the per-look Fisher information about $z$ is proportional to the centred second moment of the pass set's $k_z$ sampling,
\begin{equation}
  S_T \;=\; \sum_{i \in T} \bigl(k_z^{(i)} - \bar{k}_z\bigr)^2,
  \label{eq:kz_moment}
\end{equation}
equivalently the mean squared $k_z$ separation over all pass pairs. Repeating the budget argument with $S$ as the currency gives $W^{*} = w\sqrt{S_N/S_n}$; Eq.~(\ref{eq:floor}) is its special case for aperture-preserving subsets, whose $S$ is proportional to pass count.

Both variants share the signature that matters: the floor moves with the input stack. Across the sweep's stack sizes $n \in \{2, 3, 5, 9, 15\}$ the pass-count floor predicts knees at $76, 62, 48, 36, 28$\,px, and the $k_z$-aware variant shifts these values (for the sweep's even-spread selections, $36$\,px at $n = 5$ and $31$\,px at $n = 9$) without changing the trend. The ceiling predicts $2w = 40$\,px for every $n$. The production five-pass subset is the configuration in which the two accounts collide least --- its floor, $48$--$49$\,px, happens to sit just past the ceiling --- which is why both hypotheses remained live until a multi-$n$ sweep could separate them.

\subsection{The sweep}
\label{app:patch_sweep}

One model was trained per (patch side, stack size) cell on the grid $W \in \{16, 24, \ldots, 72\}$ and $n \in \{2, 3, 5, 9, 15\}$, with baseline-ordered even-spread pass selection at each $n$. The backbone is a three-level U-Net, so every grid size is admissible and the ceiling $2w = 40$\,px lies on the grid. The pixel budget per epoch is constant across patch sizes --- larger patches see no more data --- which removes the compute penalty of large patches by construction: the error curves saturate rather than turn upward, the argmin is censored by noise and the grid edge, and the knee is the meaningful statistic. Table~\ref{tab:patch_sweep} reports the held-out test loss.

Four observations carry the verdict.
\begin{itemize}
  \item The knee stays in the band $40$--$56$\,px --- at the ceiling or within two grid steps above it --- with no trend in $n$, while the sample-budget floors demand a knee sliding from $76$ down to $28$\,px.
  \item The ceiling already captures most of each curve: $77, 75, 100, 84, 73\%$ of the total gain is in hand at $W = 40$\,px for $n = 2, 3, 5, 9, 15$, and no curve improves by more than $0.006$ loss anywhere past it.
  \item More passes lower the entire curve without moving where context saturates: stack size buys accuracy, not context reach --- the qualitative content of Eq.~(\ref{eq:ceiling}). The budget accounts survive as information-content statements (they correctly order the columns of Table~\ref{tab:patch_sweep}) but not as predictors of the patch optimum.
  \item Of the three readings in Appendix~\ref{app:patch_ceiling}, the data select the middle one: the knee sits at $2w$, not at $w$ (the grid resolves $16$--$24$\,px, where every curve is still far from saturated), and the flat tails to $72$\,px rule out unbounded accrual.
\end{itemize}

Two caveats are visible in the table. Every curve rises from $16$ to $24$\,px before its main descent ($+0.009$ to $+0.024$ loss); the rise is shared across all five stack sizes, so it is not seed noise, and its cause is open. And the sweep is single-seed: tail wiggles of $\pm 0.005$ past the knee are within run-to-run noise, which is why knee$_{80}$ rather than the argmin carries the conclusion. \todo{Repeat the sweep over $\ge 3$ seeds and report mean $\pm$ std per cell.}

\subsection{Scope and limits}
\label{app:patch_limits}

\begin{itemize}
  \item A1 fails gracefully. Organised structure larger than a resolution cell --- building faces, slopes --- correlates samples across disjoint windows and softens the ceiling upward; it can delay saturation, not create a second optimum below $2w$. The knees observed up to $56$\,px are consistent with mild softening of this kind.
  \item The Fisher refinement prices only the second moment of the $k_z$ sampling. Vertical resolution (aperture span) and ambiguity (sampling gaps) cannot be repaid with patch area at any $W$, and per-pass SNR differences are ignored (A3).
  \item The law bounds \emph{certified} information, not what priors can add: a model may exploit scene regularities beyond $2w$ --- they are simply not guaranteed by the label construction --- and the flat tails of Table~\ref{tab:patch_sweep} indicate that on this scene such priors add little.
  \item The constants are the pipeline's own ($w$, the $k_z$ set), so the law transfers to a new scene by re-reading them from its configuration; whether the observed saturation transfers is untested (Section~\ref{sec:limitations}).
\end{itemize}
